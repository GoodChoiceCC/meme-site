Journal of Legume Phenomenology & Existential Gastronomy · Vol. 7 · Issue 3

On the Ontology of Beans:
A Treatise in Three Parts with Footnotes

By Gerald M. Porksworth¹ & Professor A. Banana (posthumously)²
Abstract
This paper examines the bean — what it is, why it is, whether it knows it is, and what it wants. Through rigorous application of post-structural gastronomy, Hegelian legume theory, and the author's personal experience eating beans at 2am over a sink, we argue that the bean occupies a unique ontological position between vegetable and philosophy. We find no conclusions. We have more questions. The beans were delicious.
"I think, therefore I am. The bean does not think. Yet the bean is. Explain that."
— Descartes (disputed; possibly made up for this article)

I. Introduction: The Problem of the Bean

There exists, in the quieter corners of philosophical inquiry, a question that has long been suppressed by the academy out of embarrassment: what is a bean? Not merely in the botanical sense — though that too remains contested[1] — but in the deeper, more troubling sense. Does the bean have an essence? Does it precede its existence? Is it both a food and a concept? Is it neither?

We submit that the bean has been catastrophically undertheorized. While Kant wrote extensively on phenomena and noumena, he did not once mention beans. This is suspicious.[2]

"If a bean falls in a forest, does it make a sound?
Yes. It makes a bean sound. This is axiomatic."

The present treatise attempts to correct this omission. We proceed in three parts: first, we address the bean's existence as such; second, we examine the bean's relationship with other beings (primarily the spoon); and third, we attempt to determine whether the bean is watching us, and if so, why.

II. Taxonomy of Bean-States

Not all beans are equal, and it would be philosophically irresponsible to pretend otherwise. After extensive classification — involving spreadsheets, a whiteboard, and one regrettable episode with a tin of kidney beans — we propose the following typology:

The Soaking BeanAwakeningMediumTo become. To transcend. More water.

The Bean You Forgot AboutTranscendentUnknowableIt has already moved on. You are the one still thinking about it.

III. The Spoon Problem

Much has been written about the spoon[3], but never in relation to the bean. This oversight is, frankly, shocking. The spoon and the bean exist in a state of profound mutual dependency — the spoon without beans is purposeless; the bean without a spoon is lost.[4]

We do not have a conclusion for this section. It is simply a thing we noticed.

IV. The Question of Bean Consciousness

In 2019, during a period of severe academic boredom, one of the authors placed a bean on a table and observed it for six hours. The bean did not move. It did not communicate. It did not demonstrate any outward sign of awareness. And yet — and this is crucial — it felt like it knew.

We are unable to publish the full methodology here due to institutional review board concerns and also because we lost the notes. However, we feel confident stating: the bean knows. It has always known. It knows about you specifically.[5]

"The bean does not fear death.
The bean IS death.
(We may have overstated this.)"

V. Conclusions (We Have None)

After three years of research, two retractions (pending), and one very confusing dinner party, we are prepared to state our findings: we do not know what a bean is. We are no closer than when we started. The bean remains, as ever, inscrutable.

We recommend further study. We also recommend eating beans, which are nutritious and filling, regardless of their metaphysical status.

This work is dedicated to the bean we soaked in 2021 and then forgot about for a month. You were the bravest of all of us.
